Kedarnath Darshanam

The Sacred Land of Lord Shiva

History of Kedarnath Temple

Kedarnath Temple, located in the majestic Himalayas of Uttarakhand, is one of the twelve Jyotirlingas of Lord Shiva and one of the holiest Hindu shrines. The temple is believed to have been originally built by the Pandavas after the Kurukshetra war to seek forgiveness from Lord Shiva. The present temple structure is said to have been re-established by Adi Shankaracharya in the 8th century CE.

The temple stands at an altitude of 3,583 meters (11,755 ft) near the Mandakini River, surrounded by snow-capped peaks. Despite facing extreme weather conditions, it has stood strong for centuries — a living symbol of faith and resilience.

Spiritual Significance

The Kedarnath Temple is dedicated to Lord Shiva, who is believed to have appeared here in the form of a conical linga, representing his hump. According to mythology, when Lord Shiva tried to avoid the Pandavas, he disguised himself as a bull. When the Pandavas recognized him, he vanished into the ground, leaving behind his hump at Kedarnath.

Every year, thousands of devotees undertake the difficult but spiritually fulfilling journey to Kedarnath, seeking divine blessings and inner peace.

How to Visit

The nearest town to Kedarnath is Gaurikund, which is connected by road from Rishikesh, Haridwar, and Dehradun. From Gaurikund, pilgrims have to trek around 16 km to reach the temple. Helicopter services are also available from Phata, Sersi, and Guptkashi.

Best Time to Visit

The temple opens for devotees around April or May each year and closes in November to February due to heavy snowfall. The best time to visit Kedarnath is between May and June, or in September and October when the weather is clear and pleasant.
